About Me

Former physicist, with a PhD in theoretical physics and applied maths, I have built up considerable experience in research, ranging from black holes and quantum computing to communication through the atmosphere and hydrodynamics.

I'm now transitioning towards the world of data. With a deep understanding of data analysis, modeling, machine and deep learning, I love to "give data a voice", explore patterns hidden from our eyes, and transform them into intepretable and operational levers.

I am particularly fascinated by AI applications in healthcare, pharmaceutical research, and oncology, where turning data into meaningful insights can accelerate drug discovery, improve diagnosis, and enable more personalized treatments. I am convinced that artificial intelligence can help us make tremendous progress and improve human life.
Over the years, I have developed a sharp ability to analyze and resolve complex problems. I have broadened my expertise to include machine learning, deep learning, and other advanced analytical tools. But, what matters the most is reasoning, a skill that extends much beyond physics and research and applies to any domain.

Data are everywhere.

They just need us to unlock their secrets.

Most recent works

Domain shifts mitigation in medical cell datasets

We propose in this project a new pipeline that relies on pretrained foundation models augmented with a preprocessing method to "re-align" medical cell datasets coming from different sources. This novel approach greatly improves generalization across data and enhances cell classification results.

paper in progress...

CMI sensors | Kaggle competition

A challenge proposed by the Child Mind Institute and the Healthy Brain Network. The goal was to detect repetitive gesture behaviors from time series sensor acquired by a wrist-worn device. These behaviors are connected to obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), and thus represent key indicators of mental health challenges.

Results: 186th (2657 teams)

AI and Quantum Computing

Is it possible to build a quantum computer with light? Yes. Is it better than a classical computer?

In principle, yes! But, there is a condition : light needs to be in a very specific quantum state. However it is very difficult, if not impossible, to assess it experimentally. In this project, we used a neural network to detect such fondamental state of light only from fragmented and measurable experimental data. It was astonishing for us to see how accurate the AI model was at this task.
